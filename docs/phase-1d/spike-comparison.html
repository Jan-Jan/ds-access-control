1. Executive summary

Two local-first stacks (p2panda, Keyhive) were independently evaluated against six substitution gates required by the ODS design (stable-ID ACL with resolver-driven keys, library-native mutation interception, CGKA rotation on trie change, organisation-as-pseudo-group, p2p connection policy, plus the WASM / no_std compile gate). Both libraries are viable Phase 3 substrates. The differences are bounded but real, and they favour Keyhive on most axes — with one significant caveat (alpha stability).

Headline. Keyhive has a −31 % lower WASM-included burden than p2panda (22 vs 32) and requires zero forks for any gate. The biggest single saving is gate 0 (no forks vs one Large fork of p2panda-sync). The second largest is gate 4 org-as-pseudo-group (Soft vs Hard).

Counterweight. p2panda is at 0.5.2 with an audit-in-progress; Keyhive is at 0.0.0-alpha.3 with no audit. p2panda's API has churned in the last 30 days (bb62866 rename); Keyhive's is also moving (1116eaf transitive delegation, f162854 serialization). The burden delta is real, but Keyhive's library is materially less mature.

4. Deep dives

4.1 WASM / no_std story

The Phase 1.d brief lists "WASM compile" as a workspace convention (matching org-members). Both spikes evaluated this.

p2pandaKeyhive
Crates compiling clean 5 of 6 (core, auth, encryption, spaces, net --no-default-features) 3 of 3 (crypto, beekem, core)
Crates failing wasm32 1 (p2panda-sync — mio 1.2.0 via tokio default features) 0
Workaround in consumer Cargo.toml Dual getrandom pin (0.2/js + 0.3/wasm_js) Single getrandom pin (0.2/js)
Fork required Yes — Large (swap tokio for tokio_with_wasm in p2panda-sync) No
Inventory hypothesis Confirmed (split outcome predicted) REJECTED — keyhive_core unexpectedly clean

Why Keyhive wins. Keyhive has fewer subcrates AND a simpler dep tree. It has no published transport at the pin (Beelay is not yet released), so it cannot fail on a transport-layer std-only dep the way p2panda-sync does. The cost of Keyhive's "no transport" is paid separately (gate 5 requires a custom session model), but on the pure-compile axis, fewer transitive deps means fewer wasm-incompatible ones.

4.2 Stable-ID ACL approach

Both libraries hardwire principal identity to ed25519_dalek::VerifyingKey at the type level. Neither offers a generic-ID escape hatch at the public surface. The salvage mechanisms differ:

p2pandaKeyhive
Principal type ActorId(pub(crate) VerifyingKey) Identifier(pub VerifyingKey)
Construction from VerifyingKey Via public From<VerifyingKey> for ActorId Direct: pub field
Salvage shape materialise_actor_id + ResolverPki<R> IdAdapter (~30 LOC, single module)
Multi-layer touchpoints Spaces (gate 1) + Encryption (gate 3 PKI threading) Single touchpoint (the adapter; threaded uniformly)
Severity Hard Hard
Fix effort Small Small

Both score Hard for the same architectural reason — the delegation log holds raw VerifyingKeys, so a MemberId rotation needs an explicit cascade (driven by gate 3). The difference is mechanical: Keyhive's salvage is one module; p2panda's is two cooperating constructs.

4.3 Op interception

Both libraries expose mutation entry points that the ODS design needs to neutralise (only the on-chain trie is authoritative).

p2pandaKeyhive
Auth/library-internal trait Groups (externally implementable — None) MembershipListener (post-fact — cannot block)
Store-level intercept Blocked: AuthGroupState<C> in private module Blocked: DelegationStore is concrete field
Spike's actual intercept BlockingGroups<Inner> at auth layer; fork patch for spaces Containment-via-wrapper (no fork)
Fork required Yes — one-line pub use types::AuthGroupState; No
Severity Hard Soft

Why p2panda's single line of fork matters. Under the fork-locally policy, even a one-line fork ships as a vendored copy of the affected crate. The line itself is trivial; the maintenance cost is real (pinning, re-syncing on upstream releases). Keyhive's containment-via-wrapper avoids all of that — at the cost of relying on the wrapper not leaking the inner Keyhive handle (which is a hygiene concern any production codebase would address anyway).

5.1 Running-code milestone & lazy-CGKA design

After the API-evidence comparison shipped, Keyhive's gate substitutions were implemented as running Rust code in spike-keyhive/. This converted analytical claims into executable evidence, surfaced a high-level-API observation at gate 1, and ultimately led to an ODS design commitment that resolves it cleanly (lazy CGKA, recorded below).

High-level-API observation (gate 1, 2026-06-02). Keyhive's Individual::new(initial_op: KeyOp) requires a signed KeyOp (i.e., a ContactCard), not a bare VerifyingKey. The convenience path Keyhive::add_member(Agent::Individual, ...) therefore needs the new member's prekey at add-time. p2panda has the symmetric property: manager.add_member(member_id, key_bundle) takes a LongTermKeyBundle at add-time. Both libraries' top-level "add member" entry points eagerly place the new member in the CGKA tree and so depend on having the prekey on-hand.

ODS design commitment — lazy CGKA / two-tier separation (2026-06-03). The eager-add path is unsuitable for ODS because publishing prekeys per-member to the trie would mean on-chain writes per prekey rotation. The committed design instead splits:

  • ACL tier (trie, on-chain). Records (MemberId, VerifyingKey). Updated only on onboarding and rotation/revocation. No prekey/ContactCard publication.
  • CGKA tier (per-doc BeeKEM, off-chain). Encryption-tree state, contains only currently-online members' x25519 leaf keys. Mutated per-write and per-rotation.

The flow:

  1. Grant. Alice grants bob ACL access on doc D. Trie records the delegation against bob's VerifyingKey. Bob may be offline. No CGKA mutation, no prekey needed.
  2. Come-online self-add. Bob's client sees the delegation, fetches doc D's CGKA state, generates a fresh x25519 leaf, commits a self-add to BeeKEM (signed by bob, authorised by the delegation).
  3. History transfer. Bob asks an already-authorised peer for the document's history. ODS's existing encryption-at-rest / encryption-in-transit split makes this safe: the peer has plaintext at-rest and retransmits under the current CGKA epoch (which bob is now in) — no privilege extension.
  4. Revocation. Trie revokes bob. Existing members commit a CGKA update; bob's leaf rotates out; fresh PcsKey delivers forward security on the new epoch.

This descends below both libraries' convenience APIs: Keyhive uses keyhive_core delegation log + beekem::Cgka::add directly; p2panda uses GroupCrdt + Dcgka::* directly. Both sub-crate decompositions are already available at the pinned SHAs.

Burden re-pin (gate 1). Under the lazy-CGKA design the trie carries only the long-term VerifyingKey — no contact-card publication channel needed. Gate 1's phase3_effort reverts to Small for Keyhive (it was already Small for p2panda). Keyhive total burden: 22 (delta vs p2panda −31 %). The interim 24 / −25 % figure from the 2026-06-02 evening revision is superseded.

Spike-evidence status. The spike's L2/L3 tests exercise the high-level add_member path — the mechanisms they validate (BeeKEM forward security, Agent::Group transitive resolution, MembershipListener-driven session termination) are identical under the lazy-CGKA composition.

The dedicated L3 lazy-onboarding scenario (tests/l3_lazy_onboarding.rs) demonstrates the committed design end-to-end:

  1. Alice creates a doc and writes pre-onboarding content (epoch N, alice-only).
  2. Bob comes online, exchanges a contact card, alice grants ACL + CGKA placement (epoch N+1).
  3. Alice writes post-onboarding content. Bob decrypts directly.
  4. Bob's attempt to decrypt the original pre-onboarding ciphertext fails — BeeKEM forward security in the new-member direction; old epochs were never encrypted to bob.
  5. Alice re-encrypts the pre-onboarding plaintext (which she has at-rest) under the current epoch. Bob decrypts the retransmission and recovers the original bytes.

All five invariants hold on first run; no integration finding surfaced. The test exercises Keyhive's high-level add_member entry point for the placement step; the Phase-3 production path will descend below it (keyhive_core delegation + beekem::Cgka::add directly) so the placement can be driven by the new member's own client. The mechanism being validated — BeeKEM epoch separation + plaintext re-transmission under the new epoch — is identical under both compositions.

Integration finding (gate 3 / L3 revocation). The forward-security invariant is demonstrated end-to-end: tests/l3_revocation.rs shows that after alice.revoke_member(bob_id, true, &Membered::Document(...)) + syncing the revocation + new CGKA ops via static_events_for_agent, bob's try_decrypt_content on a post-revoke ciphertext FAILS, while alice can still decrypt both pre and post content. Validates Keyhive's production-grade forward-security guarantee in real code.

Integration finding (gate 4 / L3 org). The L3 org_pseudo_group scenario confirms that Agent::Group(...) grants transitive doc access (bob, as a member of alice's org, appears in doc.transitive_members() without any explicit per-member grant) and that force_pcs_update(doc) emits a signed CGKA op without disturbing the org-keyed delegation. Validates the headline gate-4 differentiator in real code.

7. Recommendation

Both libraries are viable. Both libraries demonstrate the three ODS substitutions (stable-ID ACL, X25519 rotation, op interception) in working spike code. There is no architectural showstopper for either.

If burden is the deciding axis — pick Keyhive.

  • −31 % lower WASM burden (22 vs 32).
  • Zero forks required for any gate.
  • BeeKEM's O(log n) is a long-term scaling advantage.
  • Org-as-pseudo-group, push-based revocation, and async signer model each individually align well with ODS's expected use.

If maturity / risk is the deciding axis — pick p2panda.

  • v0.5.2 is materially more advanced than v0.0.0-alpha.3.
  • ROS audit is scheduled.
  • Production-ready transport (iroh) ships today.
  • Active engagement with Parity (the design's authoring org).

Recommended path forward

  1. Default recommendation: Keyhive, on the condition that Phase 3 begins after Keyhive reaches a tagged 0.1.0 release with at least one external audit. This aligns the burden advantage with a workable maturity story.
  2. Fallback: p2panda, accepting the WASM-specific Large fork of p2panda-sync and the spaces-layer one-line fork. Phase 3 starts immediately; library risk is lower today.
  3. Bridge option: prototype both, gating the choice on Keyhive's maturity progress over the next 3–6 months. The spike-common abstraction (MemberKeyResolver, ScenarioFixture, gap-matrix tooling) is library-agnostic by construction — Phase 3 can target either substrate behind the foundation.

The bridge option costs little. Both spike crates already exist and target the same foundation. Maintaining parallel implementations through the early Phase 3 phases keeps optionality open until Keyhive's maturity question is settled.
